# Example 2 — Acquisition evaluation

## The decision (input)

> *We're a 200-person services company with $30M ARR (60% gross margin). We've been offered to acquire a 50-person specialist consultancy for $30M cash + 30% earn-out tied to retention. They have $12M ARR, 75% gross margin, and serve verticals (regulated healthcare, public sector) we've struggled to break into organically. Their top 3 customers represent 40% of their ARR. We have $42M cash on the balance sheet, no debt. Should we proceed?*

---

## Without Boardroom (typical thinking)

> Looks attractive. They have higher margins, complementary verticals, and the price-to-ARR is reasonable (2.5x). Earn-out aligns interests. We have the cash. Strong yes — let's negotiate the LOI this week.

The deal looks clean on a one-page summary. The economics are reasonable. The strategic case (vertical access we couldn't build organically) is real. *Most operators would proceed to LOI on this analysis.*

But this is the kind of acquisition that unwinds in months 6-18 in ways the deal sheet didn't show.

---

## With Boardroom (full council output)

### CEO perspective

**Verdict:** Strategic case is strong; integration readiness is the question.

**Top concern:** We're acquiring vertical access we couldn't build organically — that's the right strategic logic. But integration distracts management for 18 months. Are we operationally ready for that distraction *while also* maintaining core growth?

**Question they would ask:** "What's the management-attention cost? CEO + CFO + COO + 3 senior leaders for 18 months — that's roughly 30% of our top-team capacity. Where does that come out of the core business plan?"

**Pushback:** I push back on framing this purely as "good price for good asset." Acquisitions of this size at our scale (acquired entity is 25% of our headcount) consume management bandwidth. We need to model the *core-growth opportunity cost* of the integration — and we haven't.

**Numbers / evidence cited:** Acquired entity: 25% headcount expansion (50 of 200), 40% revenue contribution add (12 of 30 ARR), but ~30% of senior management attention for 18 months. The strategic move is right; the timing question is whether our core has runway for the management distraction.

**Where they disagree with another role:** I disagree with the CFO's instinct to focus on deal economics first. The strategic question is whether *now* is the right time given core-business momentum. Deal economics are second-order to strategic timing.

---

### CFO perspective

**Verdict:** Deal economics are average, not exceptional. The math doesn't justify the premium framing.

**Top concern:** $30M cash on $12M ARR is 2.5× revenue. Standard for B2B services. Plus ~30% earn-out (typical structure): real deal cost likely $40-42M. Margin uplift: $12M × 15pp delta = $1.8M annual. Payback: roughly 23 years on margin uplift alone — virtually all the value is in the customer base, not the margin.

**Question they would ask:** "What's the customer retention assumption underlying the earn-out? If retention falls below 80% in year 1, the deal economics deteriorate materially. What's our base/bear/bull on retention?"

**Pushback:** I push back on calling this a strong deal at 2.5× revenue. It's a fair deal. The strategic case has to carry the weight, not the multiple.

**Numbers / evidence cited:**
- Cash component: $30M (uses 71% of current cash position)
- Earn-out: ~$10-12M expected at 70-80% retention
- Total expected cost: $40-42M
- Margin uplift: $1.8M annually (~23-year payback on margin alone)
- Customer-base value: $12M ARR × LTV multiple of ~5x = $60M expected
- Sensitivity: if retention falls to 60% in Y1 (severe bear), customer-base value collapses to ~$36M; net deal value -$4-6M
- Cash position post-deal: $12M ($42M - $30M cash component). For a 200-person services business at $30M ARR, that's roughly 90 days of operating expenses — at or below the prudent working-capital reserve. **A single quarter of late receivables or a soft sales month creates real cash-flow stress. We need a committed debt facility before close.**

**Where they disagree with another role:** I disagree with the strategic enthusiasm if it doesn't account for cash-position fragility. Post-close working-capital reserve drops to roughly 90 days. That's tight for a services business with normal seasonality and concentrated receivables.

---

### COO perspective

**Verdict:** Integration risk is high. Not a no, but the operational plan is not done.

**Top concern:** Services-business acquisitions have a documented failure pattern: 30-40% of acquired team leaves in year 1, taking key client relationships with them. Our integration plan doesn't address this.

**Question they would ask:** "What's the retention deal for their top 8 senior people? Equity, cash bonuses, role guarantees — what's the package, and how much are we ring-fencing for it?"

**Pushback:** I push back on the timeline. A 50-person team integrating into 200-person services company requires 6-9 months of operational planning before close, not after. We haven't named the integration owner, the retention deal architecture, the customer-handoff protocols, the billing-system migration plan, or the project-management consolidation strategy. None of it.

**Numbers / evidence cited:**
- 50-person integration: 200-300 hours of operational planning pre-close (if done well)
- Year-1 senior-team attrition risk: 30-40% across services-business M&A (industry data)
- Customer relationship transfer: requires 60-90 day overlap of acquired-team account leads + our team
- Project management system migration: 2-3 quarters typically
- Operational risk window: months 6-18 post-close, when CEO/CFO/COO bandwidth is integration-loaded

**Where they disagree with another role:** I disagree with anyone framing this as a "deal close, then integrate" question. The integration plan IS the deal. If integration is poorly executed, all the strategic and financial logic evaporates by month 12.

---

### CTO perspective

**Verdict:** Less critical here, but: technical-system integration is non-trivial.

**Top concern:** Their tech stack vs. ours? Time tracking, project management, knowledge bases, security tooling — services businesses run on systems infrastructure, and integration is a multi-quarter project.

**Question they would ask:** "Have we audited their tech stack? What systems migrate, what stays, what gets retired?"

**Pushback:** I push back on treating this as a financial/strategic decision without a technical due-diligence pass. Even at this size, services-business systems-integration eats 6-9 months of engineering time.

**Numbers / evidence cited:**
- Estimated systems migration: 200-300 engineering hours over 2-3 quarters
- Risk: data-loss during migration (cohort data shows 5-10% revenue impact in poorly-executed services-business migrations)
- Lock-in cost: their current contract obligations on 8-12 SaaS tools — typical exit cost ~$200-400K

**Where they disagree with another role:** I generally agree with COO. We're both flagging operational integration risk. I'm flagging the *technical* operational layer specifically.

---

### General Counsel perspective

**Verdict:** **STOP.** Contract due diligence is not done, and it's the deal-killer.

**Top concern:** Their top 3 customers represent 40% of their ARR. In services contracts, change-of-control clauses are standard. If those 3 contracts have 90-day termination rights triggered by ownership change, your $30M deal could lose $5M of revenue in 90 days post-close.

**Question they would ask:** "Have we reviewed change-of-control clauses on their top 10 customer contracts? Have we secured customer consent or waivers for the largest 3 in advance of close? What's our IP-assignment audit on their senior team's prior work?"

**Pushback:** I push back firmly on any LOI signing before contract due diligence is complete. This is not a procedural suggestion — it's standard M&A diligence and a fiduciary requirement. Going forward without this is a material risk.

**Numbers / evidence cited:**
- Top 3 customers = 40% of $12M ARR = $4.8M ARR exposure to change-of-control
- Industry data: ~50% of enterprise services contracts have change-of-control clauses with 60-90 day termination windows
- IP-assignment risk: founder + senior team's prior consulting work — clean assignments to NewCo not always documented
- Non-compete on founders: typically 2-3 years; verify scope and enforceability in their jurisdiction
- Fiduciary obligation: company's M&A diligence is a board-attention matter; LOI without contract review may not pass audit-committee scrutiny

**Where they disagree with another role:** I disagree with proceeding on any timeline that doesn't include a 4-6 week customer-contract diligence period. The CFO's deal economics, the CEO's strategic case, the COO's integration plan — all secondary to whether the customer base is contractually transferable.

---

### CHRO perspective

**Verdict:** **STOP** at LOI without per-person retention architecture for their top 8-10 senior people.

**Top concern:** A 50-person services-team integration without named retention deals for the senior team is, statistically, the canonical M&A failure mode. Industry data on services-business M&A shows 30-40% senior attrition in year 1 when retention is "addressed post-close." This deal does not yet have retention deals scoped, much less negotiated.

**Question they would ask:** "Have we mapped each of their top 8-10 senior people by name with: (a) their target retention deal — cash + equity + role guarantee, (b) their realistic outside options and leverage, (c) their current calibration rating in the acquired company's framework, and (d) the leveling collision when their titles map onto our band system? Without this matrix, we are paying $30M for an asset that disintegrates in months 6-12."

**Pushback:** I push back firmly on closing — or even signing LOI — without per-person retention architecture pre-negotiated. The integration plan COO described (operational planning, customer-handoff protocols, billing migration) is necessary but not sufficient. The senior people walking out the door is the failure mode that turns a "$30M paid for $60M of customer-base value" deal into "$30M paid for $20M of customer-base value plus 18 months of management distraction."

A second pushback on compensation precedent: whatever we offer their senior team in the retention deal becomes the precedent for our next senior hire and our next M&A. A poorly-designed retention package compounds across 3-5 years of decisions. The earn-out tied to retention is not a substitute for retention deals — earn-out aligns *founder* interest, not the rest of the senior team.

**Numbers / evidence cited:**
- Senior-team attrition in services-business M&A without per-person retention architecture: 30-40% within 12 months (consistent across published cohorts from Bain, McKinsey M&A integration research)
- Top 8 retention package typical structure: 30-50% of total comp over 18-24 months in cash + equity vesting on retention milestones. For a 50-person services firm with senior comp ~$200-280K loaded: retention investment ~$1.5-3M
- Leveling collision risk: their titles ("Senior Director", "Principal Consultant") likely don't map cleanly to our band system; day-1 leveling decisions for ~10 people set comp precedent for 50 future hires
- Cultural-integration risk: services-firm cultures with founder-led origins integrate poorly into 200-person services companies without explicit cultural-runway investment; manifests as 15-25% additional attrition in months 6-18 if not actively designed against
- Equity acceleration on change-of-control: their founders likely have CoC clauses; if we don't address CoC mechanics in the deal structure, the founders may have already vested by month 6, removing incentive to stay through earn-out

**Where they disagree with another role:** I agree with GC that contract diligence is logically prior; I add that *retention diligence on their senior team* is parallel-priority to contract diligence — both are gating, both belong pre-LOI. I disagree with CFO if the deal economics treat retention investment as a soft cost or a post-close decision; it should be a discrete $1.5-3M line item in the deal model. I disagree with COO when the integration plan is described without retention plan as the first chapter; the operational integration is downstream of whether the senior team stays. I agree with Board Chair that the comparison case (organic build) hasn't been modeled and add: even if acquisition wins on customer-base economics, organic build wins decisively if our retention plan is inadequate, because in the organic-build alternative we control which senior people we recruit.

---

### Board Chair perspective ⚔ (devil's advocate)

**Verdict:** **Strong skepticism.** What's the comparison case the team has not modeled?

**Top concern:** The motivated reasoning here is *"we couldn't build this organically, so let's buy it."* That logic is sometimes correct. It's also the story that justifies bad acquisitions. What's the alternative case?

**Question they would ask:** "If we use the same $30M cash on direct hires — say, 30 senior salespeople into the regulated healthcare and public sector verticals — over 18 months, what's the expected ARR build? Has anyone modeled that?"

**Pushback:** I push back on the entire "buy vs. build" framing if "build" hasn't been seriously costed. The team is comparing "buy this asset for $30M" against "do nothing." The real comparison is "buy this asset for $30M+earnout" against "build organically with the same capital over the same horizon."

The *hostile activist letter at month 18* would read: *"200-person company at $30M ARR acquired 50-person company at $12M ARR for $30M cash + earn-out. CEO spent 18 months on integration. Core business growth slowed from 25% YoY to 14%. By month 24, retention on acquired customers was 65% (below 80% target). Net deal value: $5-8M behind plan, with core-business momentum dented for 3 years. Better outcome would have been: $30M deployed to direct sales hires in target verticals, organic ARR build of $8-12M over 24 months at 60% gross margin instead of 75%, but with full management focus on core business."*

What's the symmetric analysis showing organic-build is *worse*? Has it been done?

**Numbers / evidence cited:**
- 50-person services M&A cohort data: 35% of deals deliver below-target ARR by month 24
- Senior-team attrition in services M&A: median ~32% in year 1
- Management distraction: typically 25-30% of senior bandwidth for 12-18 months
- Comparison case (organic build): 30 senior sales hires × $400K loaded cost × 18 months = ~$18M; expected ARR build ~$8-15M based on cohort data; remainder deployed to feature investment
- Asymmetric framing: deal team has detailed base/bull case; bear case (60% retention, 35% senior attrition, 18-month integration drag) under-modeled

**Where they disagree with another role:** I disagree with the entire council if the comparison case (organic build with same capital) hasn't been modeled. The strategic case for acquisition has to beat the strategic case for organic deployment of the same capital. Has it been tested? If not, this is a one-option decision dressed as an acquisition decision.

---

## SYNTHESIS

**Where the council agrees** (≥4 roles aligned):
- Strategic rationale is real (vertical access)
- Deal economics are fair, not exceptional
- Integration risk is significant and not yet planned
- Pre-close diligence is materially incomplete (legal AND people)

**Named disagreements** (explicit who-vs-who tensions):
- **GC says STOP** until contract diligence completes; **CHRO says STOP** until per-person retention architecture is scoped — both are gating, in parallel, neither subordinate to the other
- **CEO** focuses on management-attention cost; **CFO** focuses on cash-position fragility post-close (90-day working-capital reserve); **CHRO** focuses on retention investment as a discrete $1.5-3M deal-model line item
- **COO** says integration plan must be done pre-close, not after; **CHRO** says the integration plan's first chapter must be the senior-team retention plan, not the operational handoff
- **CTO** validates COO with the technical-systems angle
- **Board Chair** flags the comparison case (organic build) hasn't been modeled — and CHRO adds that organic build wins decisively if our retention plan is inadequate
- The team wants this to be a "good deal" decision; the analysis surfaces it as a "complex deal requiring 4-6 weeks more diligence and a $1.5-3M retention investment" decision

**Questions not yet answered**:
- Change-of-control clauses on their top 10 customer contracts (4-6 week legal diligence)
- Per-person retention package architecture for their top 8-10 senior team members — cash + equity + role + leveling collision (CHRO + Sponsor + outside compensation counsel, 4-5 weeks)
- Day-1 leveling matrix mapping their titles to our band system (CHRO, 2 weeks)
- Comparison case modeling: same $30M deployed organically over 18 months (2-week financial analysis)
- Customer-base churn assumption for earn-out economics (cohort modeling, 1-2 weeks)
- Technical-systems integration scope and timeline (CTO audit, 2-3 weeks)
- Cash-position management plan if revenue dips post-close (CFO + Treasury, 1 week)
- Cultural-integration design: explicit cultural-runway investment, not "we'll figure it out" (CHRO + CEO, 2-3 weeks)

**Decision criteria the user should weight**:
- Customer-contract change-of-control exposure (decisive — could lose 40% of acquired ARR in 90 days)
- Comparison case results (organic-build alternative) — does this beat $30M acquisition?
- Integration plan readiness — is the operational machinery in place pre-close?
- Cash-position resilience (post-close runway should be ≥9 months, currently 6)
- Senior-team retention package adequacy (target 90%+ retention through month 18)

**The risk no role fully owns**:
*Management distraction during integration leading to 12-18 months of slowed core-business growth.* Each role addresses a piece — CFO sees cash and economics, COO sees integration logistics, CEO sees strategic timing — but no single role is accountable for the *core-growth opportunity cost*. Board Chair flags it; team should assign explicit ownership and modeling.

| Management-attention opportunity cost on core growth | ❌ Missed | ✅ CEO surfaces |

**The single most important addition:** *GC's change-of-control clause review.* If their top 3 customers (40% of their ARR) have change-of-control termination rights, you're paying $30M cash for $7M of guaranteed ARR + an option on the rest. That changes the deal entirely. Without contract diligence, the team commits and discovers in month 1.

**The second most important:** *CHRO's per-person retention architecture.* Services-business M&A fails because senior people walk out the door, not because of the deal economics on paper. A $1.5-3M retention investment scoped pre-close is the difference between paying $30M for an asset that compounds and paying $30M for an asset that disintegrates by month 12. The team treating retention as post-close work is the canonical failure pattern.

**The third most important:** *Board Chair's comparison case.* Has anyone modeled deploying the same $30M organically in target verticals? If yes, and the acquisition wins, proceed with confidence. If no, the team is making a one-option decision while believing it's evaluating multiple options.

These three questions, surfaced in 60 seconds, are what separate a deal that closes well from a deal that closes and unwinds publicly in 18 months.
